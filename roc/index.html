Before you start · a quick walkthrough

How to evaluate AI

We all want AI to work well with high accuracy, and to avoid mistakes. But do you know there are two types of mistakes AI can make? Mistakes are not created equal, and this is true of any decision, not just AI.

The model above looks accurate. It labels every patient healthy and scores 92%, yet it catches no cancer at all. To evaluate AI we need to see both mistakes, and the tools for weighing them: the confusion matrix, the ROC curve, and AUC.

Step 1 of 7

What AI does: map each patient to a risk score

So what are the two types of mistakes? To answer that, first look at what AI does. Any AI model maps a patient's raw data into a risk score, a single number between 0 and 1 for how risky the patient is. It does not give a yes or no; it just produces the score.

gray = healthy patient red = cancer patient
the model sees only the score, never the truth.
Step 2 of 7

To make the score useful, set a threshold

A risk score on its own is not yet useful. To act on it, a clinician or decision-maker sets a threshold, also called a cutoff. If a patient's score is at or above the threshold, the model predicts cancer; otherwise it predicts no cancer.

Step 3 of 7

The two types of mistakes

With the threshold set, AI can make two types of mistakes. A patient who has no cancer but scores above the threshold is classified as high risk, a False Positive or false alarm. A patient who has cancer but scores below the threshold is predicted as no cancer, a False Negative or missed cancer. The missed cancer is the more serious mistake; a false alarm leads to an unnecessary biopsy and anxiety.

Step 4 of 7

The confusion matrix

Put the two mistakes together with the two correct outcomes, True Negative and True Positive, and you have the confusion matrix. This confusion matrix clearly depends on the threshold: move the threshold and all four counts change.

Step 5 of 7

A higher threshold trades one mistake for the other

If you set the threshold very high, you set a high bar: you reduce the Type 1 error, the false positive, but you increase the Type 2 error, the false negative. Set the threshold low and the reverse happens, catching almost every cancer at the cost of many false alarms. Lowering one mistake raises the other.

Step 6 of 7

Each threshold is one point on the ROC curve

That is where the ROC curve comes in. It shows, under every different threshold, how the True Positive Rate, the share of real cancers caught, and the False Positive Rate, the share of healthy patients wrongly flagged, change together. Sweeping through all the thresholds, each one becomes a point, and together they trace the ROC curve.

Step 7 of 7

AUC summarizes the whole curve in one number

The Area Under the ROC Curve, or AUC, summarizes performance across every threshold in a single number. 0.5 matches the diagonal, no better than a coin flip; 1.0 is perfect and hugs the top-left corner. Because AUC does not depend on any one threshold, it grades the model itself. For reference, mammography AI models score roughly 0.61 to 0.70.

Why this matters to a leader: choosing the threshold is a business and ethical decision, weighing missed cancers against false alarms and overdiagnosis. The same curve can also reveal fairness gaps, where a model that looks strong overall performs near-randomly for a particular subgroup.
