Before you start · a quick walkthrough

How to evaluate AI

AI turns each patient's data, such as a mammogram, into a prediction: a cancer-risk score. But how do we know the model is any good? A single accuracy number misleads. It hides which kind of mistake happens, a missed cancer or a false alarm, and it hides whether the model is fair: a model can be accurate on average yet perform much worse for some patient groups.

So we don't judge on one number. This walkthrough builds three tools, in order: the confusion matrix, to see which errors happen; the ROC curve, across every cutoff; and AUC, the whole model in one score. Then you try it.

Step 1 of 5

The model turns each patient into a score

A breast-cancer risk model reads each patient's data, such as a mammogram, and outputs one number: the predicted risk of cancer, from 0 to 1. Higher means more likely cancer.

gray = truly healthy red = truly cancer
the model only sees the score, never the truth.
Step 2 of 5

You pick a cutoff, or threshold

To turn scores into a yes/no call, you draw one line. Patients at or above it are predicted Positive for cancer; below it, Negative. There is no single “right” line; sliding it trades one kind of mistake for the other.

Step 3 of 5

Every patient lands in one of four boxes

Comparing the prediction to the truth gives four outcomes, the confusion matrix. Two are correct, TP and TN; two are mistakes. A False Negative is a cancer the model missed, the dangerous error; a False Positive is a false alarm, meaning an unnecessary biopsy and anxiety.

Step 4 of 5

Each cutoff is one point on the ROC curve

Every cutoff gives a different confusion matrix. Turn each one into two rates. TPR, on the vertical axis, is the share of real cancers caught; FPR, on the horizontal axis, is the share of healthy people wrongly flagged. A high cutoff sits at the bottom-left, with few false alarms but many missed cancers; a low cutoff sits at the top-right, catching all cancers but raising many false alarms. Sweep the cutoff and each matrix becomes a point; together they trace the whole curve.

Step 5 of 5

AUC packs the whole curve into one number

The Area Under the ROC Curve, or AUC, summarizes the model across every possible cutoff; it is also written AUROC. 0.5 is a coin flip, matching the diagonal; 1.0 is perfect, hugging the top-left corner. Because it doesn't depend on which line you drew, AUC grades the model itself. For context, real mammography-AI models score roughly 0.61–0.70.

Why a leader cares: where you draw the line is a business and ethical call: balancing missed cancers against false alarms and overdiagnosis. The same curve can also expose fairness gaps: a model that looks strong overall can be near-random for a particular subgroup.
